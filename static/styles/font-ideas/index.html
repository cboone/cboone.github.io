Late 1970s / Early 1980s Computer Fonts

Fonts from Adobe Fonts that capture the aesthetic of vintage computer logos, advertisements, and sci-fi interfaces.

Authentic Era Fonts

Microgramma

URW Type Foundry

The original "space age" font from 1952, precursor to Eurostile. Used by Commodore for their original blocky logo. Features squared letterforms with rounded corners that defined the futuristic aesthetic of the era.

1952 3 styles vintage futuristic mid-century-modern

Eurostile

URW Type Foundry

The quintessential sci-fi/tech font of the era. Used extensively in 2001: A Space Odyssey, Star Trek displays, and countless tech advertisements. Also available as Eurostile Extended (5 fonts) and Eurostile Condensed (4 fonts).

1962 10 styles sci-fi technology

ITC Avant Garde Gothic

Monotype (designed by Herb Lubalin, Tom Carnase, Ed Benguiat, and others)

Iconic geometric sans from Avant Garde Magazine. Captures the optimistic, futuristic 1970s aesthetic with its perfect circles and clean geometry. Reminiscent of 1920s Bauhaus movement principles.

1970 10 styles geometric bauhaus

OCR A

Adobe Originals (originally American Type Founders)

One of the first optical character recognition typefaces, designed to meet U.S. Bureau of Standards criteria. Perfect for that authentic computer/terminal look with its simple, machine-readable design.

1968 1 style monospace terminal

OCR B

Adobe Originals (designed by Adrian Frutiger for Monotype)

Designed to push the limits of optical readers while remaining easier for humans to read than OCR-A. A more refined take on the machine-readable aesthetic.

1968 1 style monospace terminal

Futura PT

Paratype (originally designed by Paul Renner)

The Bauhaus geometric sans from 1927 that remained hugely popular through the 70s/80s tech era. Used in later Commodore branding. Based on geometrical shapes representative of Bauhaus aesthetics.

1927 22 styles geometric bauhaus

Modern Recreations and Derivatives

DDT

Typodermic (designed by Ray Larabie)

A subtly superelliptical, neutral-sans font family explicitly inspired by Eurostile/Microgramma but designed to be less square and more readable in text blocks. Great modern alternative.

12 styles eurostile-inspired text-friendly

Orbitron

The League of Moveable Type (designed by Matt McInerney)

Geometric display face with that extended, futuristic computer aesthetic. Perfect for headlines and dramatic typographic applications with a distinctly sci-fi feel.

4 styles display sci-fi

Industry

Fort Foundry (designed by Mattox Shuler)

Utilitarian industrial sans with strong tech vibes. Features a comprehensive weight range from Thin to Ultra, making it versatile for both display and text use.

16 styles industrial utilitarian

Rigid Square

Dharma Type (designed by Ryoichi Tsunekawa)

Octagonal shape with 45-degree cuts creates a very mechanical/technical feeling. Very geometric shape but designed especially for body text and long sentences such as mechanical instructions.

14 styles octagonal mechanical

TT Autonomous

TypeType

A modern brutal technological sans serif. The basic visual characteristic is the noticeable squareness of the characters and angular internal space, capturing that computer-age aesthetic.

16 styles brutal technological
Notable Absence: Motter Tektura The distinctive geometric typeface used for the Apple II logo (designed by Othmar Motter) is not available on Adobe Fonts. You would need to source it from other font distributors.
